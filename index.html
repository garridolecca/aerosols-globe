About

Real aerosol transport from NASA GEOS-FP (Forward Processing) model. Wind vectors (U10M, V10M) are weighted by each aerosol species' surface mass concentration — particles flow where aerosols actually travel, dense where concentration is high, absent where air is clean.

Earth in Action Aerosols

An animated visualization of real atmospheric aerosol transport using NASA satellite data, global wind patterns, and GPU-accelerated rendering.

How This Map Was Created

  1. 1Data Acquisition — Wind vectors (U10M, V10M) and aerosol surface mass concentrations are downloaded in real-time from NASA GEOS-FP (Forward Processing) at 0.25° global resolution via OPeNDAP. No authentication required
  2. 2Aerosol Species — Five species are extracted: BCSMASS Black Carbon, DUSMASS Mineral Dust, SSSMASS Sea Salt, OCSMASS Organic Carbon, SO4SMASS Sulfate.
  3. 3Aerosol Flux Computation — Wind vectors are weighted by each species' normalized concentration: flux = wind x (aerosol / P95). This preserves wind direction but scales magnitude by how much aerosol is actually present.
  4. 4PNG Encoding — U and V components are encoded as Red and Green channels (0-255 mapping to [-max, +max]), with magnitude in Blue. Result: ~100 KB per species at 1 deg resolution.
  5. 5GPU Rendering — Particles are advected and rendered entirely on the GPU using WebGPU compute shaders (see below).

WebGPU GPU-Accelerated Rendering

This visualization uses the WebGPU API to harness your NVIDIA GPU directly from the browser — the same hardware that powers AI training and real-time ray tracing.

GPU Pipeline Architecture (4 passes per frame at 60 FPS):

  1. Compute Pass — A WGSL compute shader dispatches 500,000 particles across GPU workgroups. Each thread samples the wind texture via textureLoad(), decodes U/V aerosol flux, and advects its particle. Stochastic recycling creates natural density variation.
  2. Fade Pass — A full-screen triangle reads the previous frame's trail texture with reduced alpha, creating smooth particle trails via ping-pong render targets.
  3. Render Pass — Particles are drawn as instanced quads with a signed distance field (SDF) circular glow in the fragment shader. Additive blending (srcFactor: src-alpha, dstFactor: one) creates the neon trail effect.
  4. Screen Pass — The final composited trail texture is copied to the swapchain canvas, overlaid on the ArcGIS basemap with transparency.

Why WebGPU? Traditional Canvas 2D can handle ~8,000 particles before frame drops. WebGPU compute shaders process all 500,000 particles in parallel across GPU cores — a 62x increase — while freeing the CPU for map rendering. On browsers without WebGPU support, the app automatically falls back to Canvas 2D.

How to Use

  • Switch species — Click any aerosol type in the left panel to see its unique transport pattern
  • Pan & zoom — Drag the map or scroll to explore regions. Particles re-spawn to fill the view
  • Adjust animation — Use sliders to control particle count (up to 1M with WebGPU), speed, trail length, and particle size
  • Interpret colors — Brighter, denser particle trails = higher aerosol concentration being transported by wind
